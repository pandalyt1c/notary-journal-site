Read this first

Whether your state requires a notary journal turns on one distinction most lists get wrong: traditional in-person notarization versus remote online notarization, often shortened to RON.

Plenty of states that ask nothing of an in-person notary still require a journal or a secure electronic record for remote acts. The table below splits the two columns so you can read your own situation. Every entry reflects the statute or the state notary handbook as of July 2026, with the governing law in the last column. Laws change, so treat this as a starting point and confirm with your own commissioning authority.

"Recording only" means the state requires the remote notary to keep the audio-video recording of the session rather than a journal. "Required (electronic)" or "Required (e-notary)" means the mandate applies to electronic or online notaries, not to traditional paper acts.

Myths worth clearing up

Circulating lists get a few states wrong. These are the ones that trip people up:

  • Texas requires a record book for every notarization, even when you charge no fee. Summaries that call it optional are out of date.
  • Florida requires a journal only for online notaries. In-person Florida notaries have no journal mandate.
  • Nebraska is split. Paper notaries are not required to keep a journal, electronic and online notaries are.
  • Several states moved to a full journal requirement recently: Missouri in 2020, Kansas in 2022, New York, Illinois, and Delaware in 2023, Oklahoma in 2025, and Utah for commissions issued or renewed on or after May 6, 2026. If you are reading an older "your state doesn't require one" list, check its date.

A note on what this is

This page is a general reference, not legal advice. Notary laws change and vary by act type, in-person versus remote or electronic. Always verify current requirements with your commissioning authority, usually your Secretary of State or state notary office, before relying on this information.
