Texas Notary Journal Requirements

Why Texas requires a record book, even for no-fee acts

← Back to Notary Journal

Current as of 2026-07-01

The short answer

Yes. Texas requires every notary public to keep a record book of official acts. The core rule sits in Government Code chapter 406, with Texas Secretary of State guidance filling in the practical detail.

Unlike some states, Texas does not carve out an exception for notarizations where you charge no fee. If you perform the act, you log it. A common but outdated claim is that the record book is optional for a solo notary doing occasional favors; it is not.

What every entry must contain

Texas asks for the core facts of the act:

  • The date and type of each official act.
  • The document's date and the kind of document notarized.
  • The signer's name and address.
  • How you identified the signer.
  • The fee you charged, including a "no fee" entry when you waived it.

What the record book cannot contain

Texas forbids recording a signer's identification number, whether that is a driver's license number, a state ID number, or a passport number. Some older templates and out-of-state journal formats include an ID-number column; in Texas, leave it blank or use a format that never asks for it. This is a privacy protection, not an oversight, so it is worth checking any paper book you buy before you start using it.

Paper or electronic

Texas authorizes electronic record-keeping alongside the traditional bound book. If you perform remote online notarization, the RON platform's electronic journal has its own 5-year retention rule. For paper, in-person acts, the statute does not set a specific retention length the way some other states do, so the safest practice is to keep the book for as long as you hold the commission and beyond, consistent with the general recommendation from the Secretary of State.
